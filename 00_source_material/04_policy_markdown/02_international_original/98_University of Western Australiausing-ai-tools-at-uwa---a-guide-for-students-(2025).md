Page 1 Introduction 
What is Artificial Intelligence (AI)? 
Artificial intelligence (AI) refers to digital systems that are capable of performing 
complex tasks that previously required human intelligence to complete, such as 
learning, reasoning, information synthesis, analysis, and problem -solving. 
AI tools have been used to support learning at universities for many years. Spell - 
checkers, email spam filters, search engines, speech -to-text tools , and 
recommendation systems all use AI algorithms to help us get things done at 
university . 
 
What is Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI)? 
Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) is a subtype of Artificial Intelligence (AI) 
that uses deep l earning algorithms to generate new content from existing 
sources. GenAI creates new content in response to user inputs called ‘prompts.’ 
Recently , GenAI tools such as ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, Claude , and Google 
Gemini have become prominent and commonplace for use at universities.

Page 2 GenAI tools have many legitimate uses to help us learn and study. A 
fundamental principle that guides what we all do at university is another type of 
AI: Academic Integrity . At UWA we think of this as acting with the values of 
honesty, trust, fairness, respect and responsibility in learning, teaching , and 
research. When using AI tools at university we need to act in accordance with 
these values. 
 
Using AI Tools at UWA 
 
Students must follow their unit coordinators’ guidance on the permitted uses of 
AI tools for completion of coursework and assessment s. Guidelines for each unit 
and assessment may be different, so it is important that you read each of your 
unit/assessment outlines to understand what is required and permitted . 
 
Can I use AI tools for research and study? 
 
UWA’s Academic Integrity Policy allows for the limited use of AI as educational 
and study tools. You can use AI to assist in your research , study , and for broad 
editorial assistance in your writing, subject to the explicit permission of your unit 
coordinator or coordinating supervisor (in the case of higher degree by research 
students) . Importantly, the policy stipulates that AI tools must not be used as a 
replacement of your critical thinking and analysis skills . 
 
 
Can I use AI tools to complete my assessments? 
Permitted u ses of AI tools to complete assessments varies from unit to unit and 
assessment to assessment. You are required to follow your unit coordinator ’s 
guidance on the permitted uses of AI for each assessment item . Your unit 
coordinator will outline acceptable and unacceptable uses of AI for each 
assessment using AI-use tiers and/or AI-use statements in your unit and 
assessment outlines. Make sure to read your unit coordinator’s guidelines 
carefully to understand what is expected .

Page 5 Tier 1 – No AI 
 
If your assessment is assigned Tier 1, you are not permitted to use AI in any way 
to complete the assessment . In Tier 1 assessments, security is essential to assure 
your learning. Therefore, any use of AI for Tier 1 assessments constitutes 
academic misconduct . There may be controls such as invigilation or proctoring 
put in place for Tier 1 assessments to ensure secure conditions are maintained . 
Students may still use AI tools to support their research, study, and revision in 
preparation for a Tier 1 assessment. Please refer to your unit coordinator for 
guidance on the appropriate use of AI tools for this purpose. 
 
Tier 2 – AI assistance 
 
If your assessment is assigned Tier 2, you may use AI tools for limited and 
specified purposes outlined by your unit coordinator to complete the 
assessment . In Tier 2 assessments, AI tools can be used before or after your input 
to assist you to complete the assessment. This means that the majority of the 
work should be completed by you, and AI tools may be used as specified by 
your unit coordinator for assistance . 
Some examples of Tier 2 assessments are outlined below, but please note that 
there may be other e xamples that you encounter in your units . It is important to 
review your unit and assessment outlines to understand what AI use is permitted 
and not permitted by your unit coordinator. 
➢ Example 1 – AI use permitted for idea generation and brainstorming 
In some Tier 2 assessments, you may be permitted to use AI tools to help you 
brainstorm and generate ideas for your assessment. Your unit coordinator may 
outline specific AI tools that you are permitted to use, or otherwise may allow 
you to use any suitable tools of your choos ing. 
Additionally, your unit coordinator may allow you to use AI for brainstorming and 
idea generation throughout the entire assessment, or only for specific sections of 
the assessment.

➢ Example 2 – AI use permitted for research assistance 
In this type of Tier 2 assessment , you may be permitted to use AI tools to support 
your research . Several AI tools exist tha t specifically support academic research , 
such as Perplexity AI, Zotero, and Scopus AI. You may be permitted to use these 
tools to search, review, summari se, and organis e research content , and/or to 
collate and present your bibliography. 
While it is helpful to use AI tools to find relevant sources and sort through a large 
amount of content quickly, AI tools can sometimes make up sources themselves 
(called ‘hallucination’) or misinterpret their conclusions. Because of this, you 
should still read and assure the authenticity of each source AI tools refer to. 
Additionally, AI tools are limited in their ability to critically analyse and compare 
sources, so you still need to read and analyse each source to ensure the 
summary and claims AI makes about each source are accurate . This also 
enables you to develop deeper connections between sources , which is 
something AI tools struggle to do. 
Many AI research tools track and store libraries of r eference sources that have 
been cited as part of its search. Make sure to keep a record of these sources , 
any prompts you used, and outputs that were produced in case you need to 
reference them as part of your submission.

➢ Example 5 – Student critical evaluation of AI output 
In this type of Tier 2 assessment, you may be permitted to use AI tools to 
complete a task or section of a task, after which you will be required to critically 
analyse and evaluate the output generated. As an example, you might be 
permitted to use AI to complete a data analysis of a given dataset, after which 
you might be required to a nalyse and critically evaluate the output produced 
by the AI tool you used. 
In this type of Tier 2 assessment, you will be assessed primarily on your analysis 
and critical evaluation of the output generated by AI . However, you may still be 
required to reference and submit the AI generated outputs on which you base 
your critical analysis, and unless your unit coordinator specifies otherwise, you 
should not use AI tools to complete the critical evaluation itself. 
 
Tier 3 – Fully embedded AI 
 
If your assessment is assigned Tier 3, you may use AI throughout your assessment 
to support your own work. When completing Tier 3 assessments, you are enabled 
to take a collaborative approach with AI tools, and may use them in a variety of 
ways to complete your assessment. H owever, this does not mean that AI can be 
used to complete your entire assessment without your input and evaluation . You 
are still required to demonstrate your learning and you are fully responsible for 
the content of your assessment . Your unit coordinator will outline how they 
expect this to be evidenced. 
As an example, you may encounter a Tier 3 assessment in the form of a final 
project for your unit or degree. Depending on the assessment ’s outline and AI -
use statement, Tier 3 permits you to use AI tools in authentic and professionally -
relevant ways in order to plan, complete, and present your assessment.

How do I write effective prompts for AI tools? 
 
How do I write effective prompts for AI tools? 
 
All outputs generated using GenAI begin with a prompt . The quality of the 
prompt that you use will determine the quality of the output you receive. 
Depending on the level of sophistication and detail you are looking for, you will 
need to construct your prompts with enough information and specific 
parameters to ensure the AI tool you are using generates a useful and accurate 
response. 
 
The UWA Library has created a detailed guideline for prompting AI tools 
effectively called PROMPT -ED. You can learn more about PROMPT -ED here .

Page 11 How do I reference and acknowledge the AI tools I’ve used with 
permission in my assessment? 
 
First, you must check how and when you are permitted to use AI tools in your 
assessment; ensure you check your unit outline and assessment guidelines 
carefully for instructions. If you have paraphrased or directly quoted AI -
generated content, you will need to provide an in -text and end -text reference 
as you would when citin g any other sources; this includes both text and image 
outputs. 
 
You cannot submit for assessment any work that is not your own. Under UWA’s 
Academic Integrity Policy, plagiarism is defined as occurring when “the source 
of paraphrased material is not cited; quoted material is cited as if it were 
paraphrased; and quoted material not cited”. This policy explicitly includes 
intentionally or unintentionally using AI and Generative Pretrained Transformer 
(GPT) language as if it were your own as a form of plagiarism. 
 
Check your unit outline to confirm wh ich referencing style you are expected to 
use for your unit. The Library has guides for all the referencing styles used at UWA 
and provides guidance on how to reference and acknowledge AI outputs. If 
you have any questions about referencing visit the AI Guide: Acknowledge & 
Reference AI , or the Referencing at UWA guide for general referencing support. 
You can also ask a librarian here. 
 
 
Academic misconduct 
 
Improper use of AI -generated material in assessment can be grounds for 
academic misconduct. For assessments where the unit coordinator has explicitly 
permitted the use of AI tools, UWA’s Academic Integrity Policy states that there 
are several things you must also do when using AI -generated material: 
 
i. it must be appropriately cited and referenced, along with any other 
sources used; 
ii. it must be clear what AI tool was used and where in an ass essment AI-
generated material is used;

Remember, there is no time limit on when an occurrence of academic 
misconduct can be detected. This means that even if improper use of AI i s not 
detected at the time of submission, it can be in the future, and misconduct 
penalties can be applied retrospectively, including after you graduate. 
If you are unsure about whether you are doing the right thing, you can speak to 
the Academic Skills Centre or ask your unit coordinator or tutor. 
 
 
Privacy , copyright, and AI 
 
Students at UWA should not input any personal data or information into any 
public AI systems (such as ChatGPT, Google Gemini, and other similar systems) . 
This is because m ost AI tools will retain user inputs for future use as training data, 
as well as for other purposes. Once you input your own data or personal 
information into an AI system, you may lose control over who has access to it 
and how it is used. It is also important that you do not input any intellectual 
property or copyright material that is not your own, including the intellectual 
property of UWA or its staff – such as lecture slides, handouts, and unit reading 
material – as it is not yours to d istribute. 
 
Additionally, not all AI platforms are safe to use. The Department of Home Affairs 
has raised concerns on the safety and security of data shared with the AI tool 
DeepSeek . As such, UWA staff and students must not use DeepSeek in any way . 
UWA has blocked the use of DeepSeek on all University -managed networks and 
devices, and students should avoid using DeepSeek on their personal devices.

ins, consult ations, workshops, after -hours advice , and online resources on the
