Plagiarism and generative Artificial Intelligence (genAI)

Generative artificial intelligence technologies are used in the various phases of the research, writing and publication process in everyday academic life and in the creation of scientific texts. ETH Zurich supports the use of such tools in teaching and learning, provided that they are used responsibly and in compliance with scientific integrity.

Generative AI and AI-based tools are trained using huge amounts of data. However, many sources have not yet been digitised and are not accessible online. Therefore, ask your supervisor, other people in your department or the ETH Library about alternative sources and how to access them.

Below you will find information on ethical and responsible behaviour, as well as on plagiarism.

  • Ethical use of generative AI and AI-based tools
  • Responsible authorship
  • Transparent declaration

Ethical use of generative AI and AI-based tools

From a legal perspective, nothing has changed with regard to scientific integrity and plagiarism. This means that:

  • copyright law and personal rights
  • data protection and information security
  • ETH Zurich's guidelines on scientific integrity

remain legally binding and valid.

What does this mean for you as an author?

  • Carefully check whether information is protected by copyright, is private or is confidential. If any of these aspects apply, the information is subject to copyright law or data protection. Do not feed such information into a generative AI or AI-based tool, or ensure that you use a closed tool, ideally one licenced by ETH Zurich.
  •  Follow the Guide for Users of AI at ETH Zurich issued by the Information Security Office. 
  • Any text you enter into generative AI or AI-based tools may be stored and reused by those tools. Therefore, you should consider whether you want to submit entire passages of your text to a tool for further use. Particular caution is required with (unpublished) research results and patents! It is also ethically incorrect to enter text as part of peer review processes. Therefore, you should make a conscious decision about what information and data you enter (this also applies to illustrations, videos, audio, etc.).
  • Research projects and scientific articles are often carried out or written in collaboration with other authors. It is important that you agree at an early stage whether and which generative AI and AI-based tools may be used for which tasks and text sections. This also includes deciding whether text passages or data created as part of the project can be entered into the relevant tools and whether prompts should be recorded.
  • Generative AI and AI-based tools are generally trained using freely available works. However, some providers also use copyright-protected content or content that is only accessible behind paywalls. Nevertheless, the output generated by these tools is not covered by copyright. This means that, as the author, you can be held liable if the output you reuse infringes the rights of third parties. This applies to text as well as images, video, audio, etc.

Licensed content

The use of generative AI and AI-based tools raises many further legal questions: Are you allowed to enter content from scientific sources licensed for ETH Zurich into such tools? Are you permitted to share full texts, journal articles, books, book chapters or other data licensed by the ETH Library with these platforms? In the ETH Library handbook, under the heading “Artificial intelligence: Use of licensed full texts in generative AI tools”, we have compiled the terms and agreements with various publishers.

 

Citing and avoiding plagiarism

Output produced by generative AI and AI-based tools cannot be attributed to any author. These tools use existing texts or other information to generate new outputs based on your prompts. In terms of external page copyright law (Article 2), this does not constitute a new work, which is why these tools cannot be considered as authors or co-authors. Furthermore, as this output is neither reproducible nor reliable, generative AI and AI-based tools are not eligible and citation-worthy sources.

Generative AI and AI-based tools do not intentionally commit plagiarism or ghostwriting. The use of output by you as the author does not constitute plagiarism either. However, it should be noted that generative AI and AI-based tools do not (reliably) identify texts that have been copied verbatim or paraphrased as citations, and the output may be very close to the original. This may result in a match when checked by plagiarism detection software.

In the rare event that you copy output one-to-one into your own work, you must ensure that you correctly cite the reference according to the citation style you are using. You can find information on this on the website of the citation style you are using. Alternatively, consult the website external page Cite Them Right, which is licensed by the ETH Library.

Further information on different types of plagiarism and sources, as well as on citation and citation styles, can be found on the website Prevention of Plagiarism and in the ETH Zurich Citation Guidelines.

Responsible authorship

Taking responsibility means that, as an author, you critically review every output produced by a generative AI or AI-based tool and verify the accuracy of (supposed) facts, sources, references, links, citations, citation styles, etc. This helps minimise the risk of incorporating false, incomplete or misleading information and references, or scientific, ethical, racist, sexist, cultural, political or historical biases into your own work.

As an author, you remain responsible for your research, your information retrieval and your scientific text. Therefore, you must be involved in the development of the manuscript and make "[...] a significant scientific contribution to the planning, execution, control or evaluation of the research through personal work [...]" (ETH Integrity Guidelines, RSETHZ 414, Section 3, Art. 14). Generative AI and AI-based tools cannot meet these criteria for authorship. This means that, as an author, you must continue to conduct your scientific research and writing through your own efforts, and you may only use generative AI and AI-based tools for supportive purposes.

Transparent declaration

For students and doctoral candidates

Before you start writing your scientific text, you must clarify with your supervisor whether and to what extent you are permitted to use generative AI and AI-based tools. At ETH Zurich, students must enclose a signed declaration of originality with their written thesis. This also includes disclosure of the use of artificial intelligence technologies.

  • Bachelor's and Master's students must include a declaration of originality with every written thesis (and, depending on the case, also with every semester paper).
  • Doctoral candidates declare that they have written their doctoral thesis independently as part of their registration for the doctoral examination.

The agreement with your supervisor is decisive

  • Should the declaration be included in a table or as a descriptive text?
  • In which part of the thesis should the declaration be included (methods, appendix, acknowledgements or elsewhere)?
  • Should the chat history, prompts or the generated text also be added to the thesis as an appendix or additional material?

For scientists

This naturally also applies to academic work beyond your studies: make sure you know and follow the guidelines of your research funding agency (e.g. the Swiss National Science Foundation, SNSF), department, discipline, publisher or journal. If necessary, you should also consult with your co-authors.

In its guidelines on generative AI, ETH Zurich recommends that both students and researchers declare their use transparently: Which tool did you use, when, for which tasks and for which parts of your work?

There are tools available for creating declaration tables, such as the external page AI Usage Cards service provided by the University of Göttingen.

If the use of generative AI and AI-based tools is not declared, this constitutes a special form of ghostwriting (see section "Ethical use of generative AI and AI-based tools"). An exception to this is an explicit agreement with your supervisor that you will not declare their use. However, if there is no written agreement and tools are not declared, this constitutes deception of the readership with regard to the origin of text, graphics, images or other information. This may lead to disciplinary proceedings as per the external page ETH Zurich Ordinance on Disciplinary Measures. Detailed information on disciplinary proceedings is provided on the Disciplinary affairs website.

What else you should bear in mind

Generative AI and AI-based tools do not "know" things and cannot "think" critically, but rather generate output based on probabilities: Facts and fiction become mixed up, which can result in the output being incorrect, incomplete, misleading or distorted. This is referred to as hallucinations.

Hallucinations can also lead to non-existent or incorrect references. You should therefore use generative AI-based tools developed specifically for scientific research when conducting your literature search. A selection of suitable research tools can be found on the ETH Library website under the heading “research” and for individual disciplines under “media sorted by subject”. In contrast to open generative AI and AI-based tools, subject-specific AI tools also list sources that have not been published as open access.

Hallucinations can also occur in direct quotes. Therefore, carefully check any direct quotes suggested by the AI-based tool you are using for the following aspects:

  • Does the direct quote exist?
  • Is the wording correct?
  • Is the quote from the indicated author?

False fluency is a phenomenon whereby a text reads so well that you, as the reader, do not notice that content is incorrect or missing. This risk increases if you are not fluent in a language.

If you are not writing your work in your native language and are using a translation tool, please note the following:

  • Check translated technical terms carefully.
  • If you have machine translated paraphrased sections from third-party sources, check carefully that the original core message is still present.
  • Declare the use of generative AI and AI-based tools that you have used to assist with the translation.

When using tools to check spelling and grammar, there are a few things to keep in mind:

  • Declare these tools as well. It is possible that American and British English are mixed, for example. Declaring the tool can explain the use of different types of English. In addition, by declaring it, you reveal why you "suddenly" have perfect language skills.
  • Make sure that the tool has not made any unintended changes to direct quotes (if your work contains any). Otherwise, there may be a risk of plagiarism.

In the self-study course AI-based tools for scientific writing and research on the Moodle platform, you will find further information on topics such as technical background, reading and researching (the course is available in English).

The self-study course Ready for Take-off: How to start your bachelor's and master's thesis on the Moodle platform provides further important information on writing with generative AI and AI-based tools (the course is available in English and German).
